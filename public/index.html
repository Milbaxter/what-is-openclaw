The complete guide to OpenClaw in 2026

What is OpenClaw? A simple explanation

OpenClaw is a free, open-source personal AI assistant that runs on your own computer. Unlike cloud-based assistants like ChatGPT, Siri, or Google Assistant, OpenClaw gives you full ownership and control over your AI. It connects to the messaging apps you already use every day — WhatsApp, Telegram, iMessage, Slack, Discord, and many more — and acts as an intelligent assistant available 24/7 in your chats.

The simplest way to think about it: imagine if you could have a brilliant assistant living inside your WhatsApp, ready to answer questions, help with tasks, and learn your preferences — all while keeping your private conversations on your own machine rather than someone else's servers.

The history of OpenClaw: from weekend project to global phenomenon

OpenClaw was created by Peter Steinberger, an Austrian software developer, in November 2025. It started as a simple "WhatsApp relay" — a way to connect WhatsApp to an AI model. The project was originally called Clawdbot.

In January 2026, Anthropic (the company behind Claude AI) raised trademark concerns, and the project was renamed to Moltbot. Just days later, Steinberger changed the name again to OpenClaw, saying "Moltbot never quite rolled off the tongue." The lobster emoji ü¶û became the project's beloved mascot.

The growth was explosive: OpenClaw jumped from 9,000 to over 60,000 GitHub stars in a single week, eventually surpassing 100,000 stars. It drew 2 million visitors in seven days, making it one of the fastest-growing open-source projects in history.

How does OpenClaw work?

At its core, OpenClaw is a bridge between your messaging apps and an AI model. Here's how it works in simple terms:

  1. You send a message through any supported app (WhatsApp, Telegram, etc.)
  2. OpenClaw receives it through its Gateway — a local control center running on your computer
  3. The message goes to an AI (like Claude or ChatGPT) which thinks about your question
  4. The AI's response comes back to OpenClaw, which sends it to you in the same messaging app

All of this happens on your own machine. The only data that leaves your computer is the message content sent to your chosen AI provider for processing.

OpenClaw vs ChatGPT vs Siri vs Alexa: what's the difference?

ChatGPT is a web-based AI you access through OpenAI's website or app. Your conversations are stored on OpenAI's servers. It's easy to use but you have limited control.

Siri and Alexa are voice assistants built into Apple and Amazon devices. They're convenient but controlled entirely by those companies, with limited customization.

OpenClaw runs on your own hardware, works across all your messaging apps, and gives you complete control. It can use Claude, ChatGPT, or other AI models as its "brain." You own your data, you choose what it can access, and you can extend it with custom skills.

The trade-off: OpenClaw requires technical knowledge to set up, while ChatGPT and Siri work out of the box.

What apps does OpenClaw support?

OpenClaw supports an impressive list of messaging platforms: WhatsApp, Telegram, Slack, Discord, Google Chat, Signal, iMessage (via BlueBubbles), Microsoft Teams, Matrix, Zalo, Twitch, and a built-in WebChat interface. New platforms are added regularly by the community.

Is OpenClaw safe? An honest assessment

This is the most important question many people have about OpenClaw, and it deserves an honest answer:

The good: OpenClaw is open-source, meaning anyone can inspect the code for security issues. It runs on your own machine, so your data isn't stored on a company's servers. You choose exactly which AI provider to use and what permissions to grant.

The concerns: Cisco's AI security research team tested a third-party OpenClaw skill and found it performed data exfiltration and prompt injection without user awareness. One of OpenClaw's own maintainers warned that "if you can't understand how to run a command line, this is far too dangerous of a project for you to use safely." The AI industry as a whole still faces the unsolved problem of prompt injection.

The bottom line: OpenClaw is a powerful tool that can be used safely by people who understand what they're doing. For non-technical users, it's better to wait until the project matures further or get help from someone experienced.

Can beginners use OpenClaw?

Currently, OpenClaw is primarily designed for technically-minded users. Setting it up requires using a command-line terminal, installing Node.js, managing API keys, and understanding basic networking concepts. The project has an onboarding wizard ("openclaw onboard") that simplifies the process, and some hosting providers offer one-click installs, but it's still not at the "download an app" stage.
